Problem and Contribution Statement
Path planning is an important aspect of any artificial intelligence that controls units and moves them efficiently between areas in an environment. In particular, path planning with the use of influence maps is important because games that require the movement of units often also have areas of the map that the player should avoid, be that enemy units with a range of fire, units with an area of control, or other obstacles. In Starcraft 2, Blizzard implemented a relatively simple path-finding algorithm to move groups of units towards a waypoint, but there is no extra functionality to avoid enemy units such as what our path-finding algorithm will implement (most likely intended to add to the challenge of moving a group of units and having to account for enemy positioning). In our research for this project, we have found similar concepts implemented for other RTS games, including the original Starcraft. For instance, Uriarte and Ontanon from Drexel University researched the issue of “kiting” units with influence maps in the original Starcraft. They examined variables such as unit speed, acceleration, HP, DPS, and other game variables to determine how a friendly unit should engage an enemy unit, and run away. Another research group, Louis and Liu from University of Nevada, used algorithms for kiting and fleeing behaviors to minimize friendly unit damage and maximize enemy unit damage. They used methods such as genetic algorithms, potential fields, and influence maps to develop a successful algorithm. We will be using these papers and other sources as points of reference for our development as applied specifically to Starcraft 2.

There are a number of path-finding algorithms that could be used for this problem, such as uniform cost search, A*, or JPS. The problem of having an additional constraint in the form of an influence map is an interesting application of search as it requires an artificial intelligence to make smart choices about how and where it moves a unit or a set of units, rather than just trying to find to quickest path possible. We believe that we can modify the implementation of A* to account for this constraint, as well as expand the usage of path planning to flocking, which involves moving groups of multiple units by having many units follow a single leader who moves with a path algorithm, saving computation time. In our testing, we will compare two path-finding algorithms and determine which method is the fastest at solving this problem, as well as attempt to combine all three methods to expand pathing capabilities for units in Starcraft 2. We are basing our search algorithm off of A* because it is an algorithm we have covered in class. Since A* is commonly used for simple path finding, there are plenty of resources available for us to reference as we build our own modified version. While it is certainly possible to accomplish constraint-bound path-finding with other search methods, we believe that A* search is the most feasible way for us to complete our search implementation within the time constraint of our project. Other methods such as genetic learning algorithms or potential fields have been used to solve this approach, but they require a level of sophistication that is beyond the scope of our project.

The problem we solved in our AI did not change significantly from our proposal. A* is a well-defined path-finding algorithm, and properly addressed the movement of units over areas of a game map that have both passable and impassable terrain. Influence maps worked well for providing the functionality of avoiding enemy units and minimizing damage, although our implementation of influence maps did change over time. The concept of flocking worked well for organizing units, and the basic implementation of it addressed keeping units separated and following a leader.

Design and Technical Approach

For our path-finding solution, we propose the use of three AI techniques: A*, influence maps, and flocking. A* is a popular pathfinding algorithm that creates a path using a weighted graph based on distance values and a heuristic function. An influence map is a technique centered around the idea that certain agents in an environment have different relationships between other agents and events that happen in the environment. The strength of these relationships influences how agents move in the environment. The flocking model emulates a “follow-the-leader” mentality, where individual agents in a group of units maneuver based on the movements of their leader and surrounding agents. Our flocking model will be based off of the artificial life program Boids created by Craig Reynolds. It will follow the rules of “separation,” “alignment,” and “cohesion.” A* will address the issue of finding a path from the starting position to a goal. Influence maps and flocking will each be integrated with A* to affect an agent’s path, attempting to avoid any dangers along the way.

Our design and technical approach were altered throughout the course of the project as we learned more about the technical details of the s2Client API. In general, we initially tried to create a bot that could successfully play a game up to the point of creating marines, including building supply depots, barracks, and training more SCV’s, then rallying a group of units, waiting until a specific checkpoint, and then rallying and moving to a goal point. We quickly realized that time would not allow for this holistic approach to designing a bot, and we decided to create a custom map to show off the core functionality of the AI. We used an existing map and modified it to have a spawn point for friendly marines, with various spawn points for enemy roaches that we could avoid. Roaches were a good choice for enemies, as they have a similar range to the marines, as well as a movement speed that is just slightly slower, which should allow for our marines to dodge enemy fire given a good path-finding and avoidance algorithm.

For flocking, the design of using separation, alignment, and cohesion did not change, but the approach to using these concepts did change over time. We had to implement changes like a multiplier to use for various distance vectors to “push” units away from each other, rather than use the raw distance vector, and an update rate that determined how often marines would be given an order to move away from each other, as giving units too many move commands could lead to strange clustering behavior.

The design and approach for influence maps changed as well. Initially, cells in the influence map affected by influence sources would be altered by three parameters: momentum, decay, and frequency. Momentum dictated if the new influence value would be more biased towards the new calculated influence value or the old influence value. This parameter was taken out entirely to simplify calculations for the new influence value. The frequency parameter was also taken out, and an update map function was implemented instead. How often the map is updated is directly proportional to how often the path from A* is updated--which is determined by our bot. The decay parameter was kept however; it is simply a constant that we can change to affect how influence values decay over distance in the exponential function. A larger decay constant allows for faster decay the further away a cell is from the location of the influence source. Aside from these parameter changes, how values were rounded when calculating influence values for influence sources also affected the influence map. This created some minor errors in calculated values, but overall the influence map’s cells’ values are still effective.

Another aspect of the influence map design that changed was how the map was being updated. Initially the map would keep the values of its previous state and use that when updating, but this created some discrepancies between the map’s values and the correct values, most likely because the decay parameter would have to be calculated differently. The values of each cell affected by an influence source is cumulative, therefore the previous state of the cell would leave a “trail” of values, making the range of an influence source larger. This sort of design would have been more like a heat map, which is slightly different than an influence map. This design was implemented as well (it can be seen the influence map branch, in the /pathfinder/inf_maps directory, in previous commits), but it was ultimately discarded as it deviated from the influence map model. Therefore, instead of keeping the previous state of the influence map, the influence map was simply recalculated for every influence source whenever update map was called.

The A* algorithm, overall, is well-defined and thus mostly unchanged from the perspective of implementation for this project. It was the connection to the API that made the problem difficult, and that was something that changed over development. Due to time constraints and challenges with understanding the API, the A* algorithm’s inputs are very simplified. Given more time, more obstacles and specifications could have been added to its functionality, but our implementation only manages terrain that is “passable” or “impassable,” which of course is the cost and not the heuristic. It was particularly challenging to connect the different maps together from the AI, to find blocked tiles. The A* algorithm was coupled together with the influence maps, in order to provide the most optimal path for the traveling units. In addition to shortest path calculations and tests for passable or impassable terrain, the A* algorithm accounted for possible enemy units through the influence maps. Enemy units created an additional cost, which lessened the further away units were from the enemies. Thus, the marines traveled by the shortest path from the starting point to the goal, accounting for obstacles, unplayable area, and enemy units that could damage their health.

Tech Stack

We will be working on the Starcraft 2 AI system by using the PySC2 library and wrapper for the Blizzard protocol buffer, using the Python programming language. In addition, we will be utilizing Python testing tools to evaluate our code, which will be targeting the Linux OS.

The technology stack that we are using is the most appropriate choice as it is cross compatible with most operating systems. For our programming environment, we will be using Visual Studio Code. Along with our programming environment, we will be utilizing Github for version control. Github will be used to share files with each other and maintain documentation. Each algorithm will have its own separate branch, and our master branch will have our final algorithm. We will also have a separate branch for testing. We plan to submit our final project using Github. Since we will primarily be using Python, we will use Grig Gheorghiu’s Python Testing Tools Taxonomy to assure quality code.

We had to make several changes to our tech stack for this project. For one, we initially abandoned PySC2, as the professor told us that was more suited to deep learning than bot control. Then we attempted to set up the build environment for the Python SC2 library, but then discovered several hours in that support for Linux had been broken recently, and that the developer was still working on fixing it. Since we were on a tight deadline, we made the decision to move to our final library, the official Blizzard s2Client API, in C++.  In addition, Linux turned out to be an under-performing operating system for this project. As Starcraft 2 does not have an official Linux version, we had to run the game renderer through an OpenGL development library built into the SC2 API, which meant performing even simple tasks such as rendering UI layers or moving the camera was made much more complicated. We decided to primarily switch to a Windows platform after this, as that would allow us to run our bot on the official Starcraft 2 game client, and have full control of camera and unit movement, as well as access to other UI elements if needed. To facilitate this switch, we used Visual Studio 2017 to build and compile our project code, and we used the official Starcraft 2 client to run our executables.

Evaluation

A successful run would involve the utilization of all three techniques (A*, flocking, influence maps) to get the user’s group of units to the end goal via the best path. The best path is the lowest cost route with fewest obstacles and enemies. Overall, the path-planning algorithm should be efficient and accurate, such that a real user would find it useful. Thus, it should finish with less damage to the units than the default pathing mechanic. We will be collecting data to test these aspects by running multiple plays and analyzing the steps for time and success rate.

Through running several tests, we found the combination of our AI techniques -- A*, flocking, and influence maps -- to be successful. When using the simple move command, our units were targeted by roaches and took damage. Now, upon utilizing our bot, the marines completely avoid roaches and thus take no damage. In addition, the units go around impassable terrain or obstacles, and move using the shortest possible path (accounting for enemy units) toward the goal. Flocking also helps keep the units safe, for example, if one marine becomes targeted by a roach because there is no way of getting around the roach, the other units are at a safe enough distance away and not clumped together, so less damage is dealt to the entire group in total. A* keeps the units from bumping into obstacles or wandering into impassable terrain, keeping them within playable bounds and away from blocked tiles. In addition, it calculates the shortest path from the starting point to the end goal, making the travel time the shortest possible considering all other factors. The influence maps create a radius around enemy units to drive marines away from them as much as possible, so if there is a path available to the units that has no enemies at all, they will follow that path. This is because our success is measured by total group health, not by quickest time to the goal, so avoiding taking damage from enemy units takes precedence over taking the absolute shortest path.
